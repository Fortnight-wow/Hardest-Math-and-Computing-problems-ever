# Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer Conjecture

## The Question

Elliptic curves are equations of the form

y² = x³ + ax + b

that have played a central role in modern number theory.

During the 1960s, Bryan Birch and Peter Swinnerton-Dyer noticed a remarkable relationship between the rational solutions of an elliptic curve and a function known as its L-function.

The conjecture states that the number of rational solutions on an elliptic curve is encoded in the behavior of its L-function near s = 1.

More precisely, the rank of the elliptic curve should equal the order of vanishing of its L-function at s = 1.

## Why It Matters

The conjecture connects two seemingly different worlds:

- Arithmetic of elliptic curves
- Complex analytic properties of L-functions

A proof would unify major areas of modern number theory and deepen our understanding of Diophantine equations.

## Historical Significance

This conjecture played an important role in the development of arithmetic geometry.

It is closely related to Fermat's Last Theorem, the Modularity Theorem, and many modern developments in algebraic number theory.

## Prize

The Clay Mathematics Institute has designated it as one of the Millennium Prize Problems.

Prize amount: $1,000,000.
