# Fermat's Last Theorem

## The Question
Around 1637, Pierre de Fermat wrote in the margin of a book that the equation

a^n + b^n = c^n

has no non-trivial integer solutions when n > 2.

He also claimed that he had a proof, but no proof was ever found.

For more than three centuries the statement resisted every attempt at a solution.

## Why Mathematicians Cared
The problem looks elementary because it can be explained to a school student, yet every direct attack failed.

Over time it became connected with elliptic curves, modular forms, and algebraic number theory.

## How It Was Solved
The decisive breakthrough came from Andrew Wiles.

The strategy was not to attack Fermat's equation directly.

Instead, Wiles proved a deep modularity result for elliptic curves. Combined with earlier work by Gerhard Frey, Jean‑Pierre Serre, and Kenneth Ribet, this implied that any hypothetical solution of Fermat's equation would lead to a contradiction.

After correcting a gap in the original argument with Richard Taylor, Wiles completed the proof.

## Final Result
There are no non-trivial integer solutions to

a^n + b^n = c^n

for any integer n > 2.
